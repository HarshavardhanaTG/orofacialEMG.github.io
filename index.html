emg2speech

Geometry of orofacial neuromuscular signals: speech articulation decoding using surface electromyography

Harshavardhana T. Gowda1
Lee M. Miller1
1University of California, Davis
Paper Code Data

Abstract

We present a neuromuscular speech interface that translates silently voiced articulations directly into text. We record surface electromyographic (EMG) signals from multiple articulatory sites on the face and neck as participants silently articulate speech, enabling direct EMG-to-text translation. Such an interface has the potential to restore communication for individuals who have lost the ability to produce intelligible speech due to laryngectomy, neuromuscular disease, stroke, or trauma-induced damage (e.g., radiotherapy toxicity) to the speech articulators. Prior work has largely focused on mapping EMG collected during audible articulation to time-aligned audio targets or transferring these targets to silent EMG recordings, which inherently requires audio and limits applicability to patients who can no longer speak. In contrast, we propose an efficient representation of high-dimensional EMG signals and demonstrate direct sequence-to-sequence EMG-to-text conversion at the phonemic level without relying on time-aligned audio.

For direct EMG-to-speech conversion (i.e., EMG → audio), see our EMG-to-speech demo.

Results on emg2qwerty

Comparison between our method and emg2qwerty baselines, averaged over 8 subjects. Lower CER is better; the improvement is statistically significant (p < 0.015). LM denotes language model.

Without a language model, our method achieves an 8.8% relative improvement in CER. With a 6-gram character-level language model, the relative improvement increases to 16.8%.

For reference, in a personalized fine-tuning paradigm, the emg2qwerty paper first trained a generic model on data from 100 subjects, using nearly 100× more data, and then fine-tuned it on 8 individual subjects. This achieved a CER of 11.29% without a language model and 6.95% with a 6-gram character-level LM. The 6.95% result represents a strong performance ceiling enabled by large-scale pretraining.

In contrast, our 7.95% CER is obtained using only per-subject training, with approximately 100× less data, and is already close to this ceiling, highlighting the effectiveness of our approach. We achieve this by making a single, well-motivated change to the model architecture: by replacing EMG spectrograms with SPD matrix representations.
